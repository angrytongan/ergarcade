Comparing Ergometer Metrics

Comparing performance metrics like pace and distance across ergometers is difficult. In this article we examine using power as the base from which to equate other metrics.

The problem

There is no standard construction for an ergometer. Manufacturers choose their own design, materials, build and assembly processes. This lack of standardisation extends to the values displayed on the ergometer monitor: one manufacturer will show different values to another for the same input from the user.

Here, we propose power to equate ergometers, and why this is closer than any other metric.

A possible solution

Power is the amount of work that has occurred over a period of time. We are going to assume that if one ergometer records the same amount of work as another ergometer, over the same period of time, they are equivalent measures, irrespective of how that work was completed or calculated.

For example, if one bike ergometer takes 1 pedal rotation to generate 1 watt of power, and another takes 2 pedal rotations to generate 1 watt of power, we say they are doing the same amount of work over the same amount of time even though the mechanical action is different. This yields a naive formula of:

ErgA 1RPM = ErgB 2RPM = 1W

This assumption removes any dependency on specific hardware or software characteristics. We equate a watt recorded on one machine as the same as on another machine.

Before we delve further into the methods for comparing ergometers using power, let's examine the links between displayed metrics on a single ergometer first.

Relationships between metrics

Ergometers may have a non-linear relationship between a physical action and a metric displayed on the monitor. For example, air-resistance ergometers that use a flywheel have a cubic relationship between power and velocity. Some relationships are linear, such as RPM and distance.

The exact method of how to generate the relationship between action and displayed metric are beyond the scope of this proposal, as the number of variables involved is substantial and their relationships may be complex. Rather than attempt to discern the exact mathematics used by each manufacturer, we will base our calculations by the simpler method of collecting metrics for various user inputs.

Metric collection

We collect these metrics through two methods: derivation and observation.

Derived metrics

Some manufacturers, such as Concept2, publish their math for calculating metrics. These can be in the form of calculators, tables and formulas. For example, Concept2 calculate power as:

Powerwatts = 2.8 / (split/500)3

where split is pace over a set distance.

Q: An athlete completes 1000m on the Concept2 BikeErg in 1:40.0. What would the equivalent pace be on the Assault Air Bike to equate it to the BikeErg?

A: We need to look up the work done on the BikeErg at 1:40, and apply it to the Assault Air Bike curve to determine equivalent pace. 1:40 on the Concept2 curve of the graph is 349W. We can now look along the curve for the Assault Air Bike for 349W; the nearest value is 346W, at a pace of 1:31. So, the equivalent pace on the Assault Air Bike is between 1:30 and 1:31.

A lot of data collection

Lacking sufficient derived metric formulas and math provided by manufacturers, observed metrics would be the easiest way to collect all required values in order to provide a means to compare many ergometers. This method is time intensive, as accurate and repeatable measurements must be made for a range of user inputs on the device.

However, performing the collection would allow us to determine relationships between:

  • Rotations per minute / strokes per minute;
  • Distance;
  • Power;
  • Speed / pace;
  • Calories per hour;

and so on, not only on one ergometer type, but across ergometers from different manufacturers.

By equating metrics between ergometers, workout providers would be able to more easily provide scalings based on device metrics rather than just feel. For example, calories as a target on two different ergometers will be different, but given the right amount of data, these targets could now be calculated more accurately than by feel.

Accuracy concerns

Derived metrics are mathematically correct, so on paper our values would be highly accurate. However, in the real world these values may vary significantly. Similarly, observed metrics have their own issues and deficiences.

Manufacturing tolerances

Mass producing any machine requires balancing accuracy and precision against budget and marketability. Decisions made during design may lower tolerances during construction, yielding machines that differ from each other even though they may be built in the same location or even in the same batch. These differences may manifest as slightly different metrics for the same inputs.

Dampers

Some machines include a damper, which is used to adjust the measured rate of deceleration after an act of exertion by the athlete on the ergometer. The mechanism for damping could be adjustable air intake, friction or magnetic brake.

Adjustable dampers won't affect relationships based on power, but will affect metrics like RPM and speed. For these metrics, curves for each damper setting will need to be generated.

Wear

Product wear may impact operation and reporting of accurate figures. For example, air-based ergometers accumulate dust in the flywheel cage, and change the amount of air entering, altering the amount of drag the vanes experience. This will affect damper-based metrics.

Sample points

For observed metrics, a small number of samples will not generate highly accurate curves. For example, 4 points were used in the above graph for the Assault Air Bike over a small range of pace. The curve that resulted varies significantly to the original points at higher paces.

Environmental issues

The environment that an ergometer is used in could drastically change the metrics it reports. For example, ergometers that use air for resistance will operate differently at higher altitudes than at sea level, due to the change in air pressure.

Summary

If we consider power to be equal across ergometers, it's possible to obtain, either through derivation or observation, metrics from different erogmeters that we can compare to equate different types of machines.
